Internships and Programs

Spark SIP

Through Spark SIP, high school students will be able to connect with industry experts, educators, and mentors in disciplines like computer science. While participating in research in real-world settings, students receive mentorship from these industry leaders. Interns must be able to commit to working 30-40 hours per week for 8-12 weeks over the summer.

Last updated on February 26th, 2022
Oak Ridge Institute

Are you an undergraduate student currently pursuing a bachelor’s degree and looking for an opportunity to enhance your education in a STEM-related discipline? The Oak Ridge Institute for Science and Education administers a variety of STEM internship and fellowship programs for undergraduate students across the United States.

Last updated on February 26th, 2022
RISE

A six-week program for academically-motivated rising seniors, RISE gives students the opportunity to conduct real research under the guidance of Boston University faculty, postdoctoral fellow, or graduate student mentor. There are a number of STEM tracks available.

Last updated on February 26th, 2022
Simons Summer Program

Participate in hands-on research under the guidance of faculty mentors. In this program, you’ll learn laboratory techniques and tools, be a part of an active research team, and get a taste of college life. Apprentices also attend weekly faculty talks and participate in events. At the culmination of the program, apprentices produce a written abstract and research poster.

Last updated on February 26th, 2022
NIST

Divided into six NIST laboratories, NIST performs research in a variety of engineering and physical sciences topics. Through this program, students may work in any of these laboratories: Communications Technology (CTL), Engineering (EL), Information Technology (ITL), Material Measurement (MML), NIST Center for Neutron Research (NCNR), Physical Measurement (PML).

Last updated on February 26th, 2022
Sandia

Sandia encourages students from high school to the PhD level to tackle real-world, challenging projects that impact the nation and globe. Interns partake in projects, often at technical institutes, in disciplines like cybersecurity, software development, and more. In their roles, students will receive mentorship and have the opportunities to engage in social activities.

Last updated on February 26th, 2022

Successful Women in S.T.E.M.

Born in Mumbai, India in 1947, Uma Chowdhry specialized in the science of ceramic materials, including catalysts, proton conductors, and ceramic packing for microelectronics. She received a Bachelor’s degree in physics from Mumbai University before receiving a masters degree in engineering science in California Institute of Technology. Chowdhry continued to become the Senior Vice President and Global Chief Science and Technology Officer of DuPont despite not being interested in STEM at the beginning of her journey.

Uma Chowdhry

Chien-Shiung Wu, born on May 31, 1912 was a Chinese-American physicist who made significant contributions in the field of physics. She graduated at the top of her class and was admitted to a university in Nanjing where she majored in mathematics. Later, Wu transferred to physics and continued on to help develop the process of separating uranium metal into Uranium 235 and Uranium 238 isotopes, and also contributed to the Manhattan project. Some of her nicknames are “First Lady of Physics” and the “Chinese Madame Curie.”

Chien-Shiung Wu

Jane C. Wright, born November 20th, 1919 was a pioneering cancer researcher, and was noted for her contributions to chemotherapy. She also pioneered the use of the drug methotrexate to treat breast cancer and skin cancer. Since Rubik’s cubes were invented, Wright developed her passion for solving puzzles and learning how to solve them, which led to her interest in finding cures for cancer. Although women were a minority in the medical field, it didn’t stop her from chasing her dreams.

Jane C. Wright

An American psychologist and industrial engineer at the turn of the 20th century, Lillian Gilberth was known as the “genius in the art of the living”. Born on May 24th, 1878, she became the first female engineering professor at Purdue University and the first woman elected to the National Academy of Engineering. In college, she was the top in her class and got exemplary grades in her first year. She then continued further on her education and got admitted to the University of California.

Lillian Gilbreth

Katherine Johnson, born in 1918, helped pave the way for women of color in STEM fields by helping NASA’s first crewed spaceflight land on the book in 1969. Initially, she worked for NACA, but was segregated by the white mathematicians. Despite her struggles, she pushed through to pursue her dreams, and joined NASA’s all man-team in 1959. She calculated trajectories, launch windows, and emergency return paths for some of NASA’s most famous crewed flights, and was later awarded the Presidential Medal of Freedom by Barack Obama in 2015.

Katherine Johnson

S.T.E.M.'s Impact and Importance

Exposure to STEM education gives you skills that make you more employable and ready to meet the current labor demand. In addition to that, it also gives you the wide range of experiences and skills you will need to succeed in life. It helps you become better at research and critical thinking which you can then use to solve various problems that are going on around the world. For example, think about how the alternative sources of energies were made- they were made by innovative people that pursued STEM. Or TESLA, which transitions our world to sustainable energy in order to create less pollution. All in all, science is everywhere in the world around us, and pervades every part of our lives. For this reason, we encourage young women to pursue STEM-related fields as it carries a large impact and a great significance.
